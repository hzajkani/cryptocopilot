# Ethereum (ETH)

## Identity
Ethereum is a decentralized, programmable blockchain proposed by Vitalik Buterin in 2013 and launched in July 2015. Its native asset is ether (ETH). Ethereum generalizes the blockchain into a global, permissionless platform for smart contracts — self-executing programs that run on the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM). It is the largest smart-contract ecosystem and underpins much of decentralized finance (DeFi), stablecoins, and NFTs.

## Consensus
Ethereum uses Proof of Stake (PoS). In "The Merge" (September 2022) it replaced Proof of Work with a PoS consensus layer (originally the Beacon Chain). Validators must stake 32 ETH to propose and attest to blocks; the protocol combines the Gasper finality mechanism (Casper FFG plus the LMD-GHOST fork-choice rule) to reach finality in epochs. Validators earn rewards for honest participation and can be penalized or "slashed" for misbehavior.

## Supply schedule
Ethereum has no fixed maximum supply. New ETH is issued to reward validators, while EIP-1559 (August 2021) introduced a base fee that is burned on every transaction. When network activity is high, the burn can exceed issuance, making net supply temporarily deflationary; when activity is low, supply grows modestly. Net issuance is therefore variable and depends on staking participation and on-chain demand.

## Use case
Ethereum is a settlement and computation layer for decentralized applications: token issuance (including the ERC-20 and ERC-721 standards), DeFi protocols, stablecoins, and more. ETH is used to pay transaction fees ("gas"), as collateral in DeFi, and as the asset staked to secure the network. A rollup-centric scaling roadmap moves execution to layer-2 networks that post data back to Ethereum for security.

## Key risks
Risks include smart-contract bugs and exploits in applications built on the platform; high gas fees during congestion; complexity of the protocol and staking; maximal extractable value (MEV) affecting transaction ordering; potential validator or staking-provider concentration; and regulatory uncertainty around staking and tokens.

## On-chain footprint
Ethereum uses an account-based model. Frequently tracked metrics include daily transaction counts, active addresses, gas used and fees burned, total value locked (TVL) in DeFi, and the amount of ETH staked. Layer-2 activity is increasingly significant as execution migrates off the base layer.
