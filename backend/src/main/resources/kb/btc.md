# Bitcoin (BTC)

## Identity
Bitcoin is the first decentralized cryptocurrency, described in the 2008 white paper "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System" by the pseudonymous Satoshi Nakamoto, with the genesis block mined in January 2009. It is a permissionless, open-source network whose native asset (BTC) is used as a store of value and a medium of exchange. Bitcoin has no company, CEO, or central issuer; it is maintained by a global community of node operators, miners, and developers, with reference software historically known as Bitcoin Core.

## Consensus
Bitcoin uses Nakamoto consensus built on Proof of Work (PoW) with the SHA-256 hashing algorithm. Miners compete to find a block hash below a target; the network adjusts difficulty roughly every 2016 blocks so that blocks are produced on average every 10 minutes. The chain with the most accumulated work is considered valid, which secures transaction ordering and prevents double-spends. Security derives from the economic cost of energy and hardware required to rewrite history.

## Supply schedule
Bitcoin has a fixed maximum supply of 21 million BTC. New coins enter circulation as the block subsidy paid to miners. The subsidy halves every 210,000 blocks (approximately every four years) in an event called the halving: it began at 50 BTC, fell to 25, 12.5, 6.25, and 3.125 BTC after the April 2024 halving. This disinflationary schedule means issuance trends toward zero, after which miners are compensated by transaction fees alone.

## Use case
Bitcoin is most commonly described as "digital gold" — a scarce, censorship-resistant store of value and settlement network. It supports peer-to-peer payments and final settlement without intermediaries. Layer-2 systems such as the Lightning Network enable faster, lower-cost payments by settling channels back to the base chain. The base layer prioritizes security and decentralization over high throughput.

## Key risks
Risks include high energy consumption associated with PoW mining and the associated environmental scrutiny; limited base-layer throughput (a few transactions per second), pushing scaling to layer-2; price volatility; the long-term transition of miner revenue from subsidy to fees; and evolving regulatory treatment across jurisdictions. Concentration of mining hardware and pools is also discussed as a centralization concern.

## On-chain footprint
Bitcoin uses an Unspent Transaction Output (UTXO) accounting model rather than account balances. Commonly tracked on-chain metrics include the number of confirmed transactions per day, the count of unique active addresses, estimated transaction (transfer) volume in USD, hash rate, and mining difficulty. These reflect network usage and the cost of securing the chain.
