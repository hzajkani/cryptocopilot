# XRP (XRP)

## Identity
XRP is the native asset of the XRP Ledger (XRPL), an open-source, decentralized blockchain first released in 2012 by Jed McCaleb, Arthur Britto, and David Schwartz. The company Ripple is a major contributor to the ecosystem and uses XRP in cross-border payment products, but the XRP Ledger itself is independent open-source software. XRP is designed for fast, low-cost value transfer and settlement.

## Consensus
The XRP Ledger does not use Proof of Work or Proof of Stake. Instead it uses the XRP Ledger Consensus Protocol, a federated, agreement-based mechanism. Each validator references a Unique Node List (UNL) of trusted validators; nodes propose and iteratively agree on the set of transactions for each ledger version, reaching consensus in three to five seconds. There is no mining and no staking; validators are not directly rewarded with new XRP.

## Supply schedule
XRP has a fixed maximum supply of 100 billion tokens, all created at inception (pre-mined). There is no ongoing issuance. A small amount of XRP is destroyed (burned) as a transaction fee with every transaction, so the total supply slowly decreases over time. A large portion of XRP has historically been held by Ripple in time-locked escrow accounts that release tokens on a schedule.

## Use case
XRP is used as a bridge asset for cross-border payments and settlement, enabling value transfer between currencies quickly and cheaply. The XRP Ledger also supports a built-in decentralized exchange, token issuance, and payment-channel features. Its design emphasizes settlement speed and low fees over general-purpose smart-contract programmability.

## Key risks
Risks include regulatory and legal uncertainty — notably the prominent U.S. SEC litigation concerning whether XRP sales constituted securities offerings; concentration of holdings and the influence of escrow releases on circulating supply; reliance on the trusted validator (UNL) model, which differs from open PoW/PoS security; and competition from other payment networks and stablecoins.

## On-chain footprint
The XRP Ledger is account-based with a built-in order book. Tracked metrics include transactions per ledger and per day, active accounts, fees burned, and escrow release/return flows. Because consensus is fast and fee-burning is tiny, throughput is high and supply contracts gradually.
